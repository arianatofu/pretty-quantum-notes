Schrödinger Equation

Introduction

The Schrödinger equation is the fundamental equation of motion in quantum mechanics. It describes how the quantum state of a physical system changes over time. Wavefunctions, usually denoted ψ(x,t), contain all the information about a system.

Time-Dependent Schrödinger Equation

The time-dependent Schrödinger equation (TDSE) is:

iħ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥ ψ

Here, Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator, representing the total energy of the system, and ħ is the reduced Planck’s constant. The TDSE is used for systems that evolve over time or are in superpositions of energy states.

Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation

For many problems, the potential V(x) does not depend on time. We can separate variables and write the time-independent Schrödinger equation (TISE):

Ĥ ψ(x) = E ψ(x)

Where the Hamiltonian is usually:

Ĥ = - (ħ² / 2m) d²/dx² + V(x)

Solving the TISE gives the allowed energy levels E and corresponding stationary states ψ(x) for the system.

Probability and Normalization

The wavefunction describes probabilities. The probability of finding a particle between x and x+dx is:

P(x) dx = |ψ(x)|² dx

Wavefunctions must be normalized so that the total probability of finding the particle is 1:

∫ |ψ(x)|² dx = 1

Key Concepts

  • Superposition: Wavefunctions can be added to create new valid states.
  • Operators: Observables correspond to operators acting on wavefunctions.
  • Eigenstates and eigenvalues: Solving the TISE gives discrete energy levels for bound systems.
  • Time evolution: TDSE governs how quantum states evolve in time.
