Quantum Time Evolution

Introduction

Time evolution describes how a quantum system changes over time. The fundamental rule comes from the time-dependent Schrödinger equation:

iħ ∂|ψ(t)⟩/∂t = Ĥ |ψ(t)⟩

Time Evolution Operator

For a system with a time-independent Hamiltonian, the state at time t is given by:

|ψ(t)⟩ = U(t) |ψ(0)⟩, where U(t) = e^(-i Ĥ t / ħ)

Here, U(t) is the time evolution operator, which is unitary: U†U = I. Unitary operators preserve probabilities.

Energy Eigenstates

If the system is initially in an energy eigenstate |φ_n⟩ with eigenvalue E_n:

Ĥ |φ_n⟩ = E_n |φ_n⟩

Then the time evolution is simple:

|ψ_n(t)⟩ = e^(-i E_n t / ħ) |φ_n⟩

The probability density does not change over time for stationary states.

Superposition of States

For a state that is a superposition:

|ψ(0)⟩ = ∑ c_n |φ_n⟩

Each component evolves independently:

|ψ(t)⟩ = ∑ c_n e^(-i E_n t / ħ) |φ_n⟩

This leads to interference and time-dependent probabilities.

Key Concepts

  • Time evolution is unitary and preserves probabilities.
  • Energy eigenstates are stationary – their probabilities don’t change.
  • Superpositions evolve in time and can show oscillations or interference.
  • The evolution of observables can be computed in the Schrödinger or Heisenberg pictures.

Summary

Understanding time evolution is essential for predicting how quantum systems behave, from simple particles in a box to atoms, molecules, and quantum computers!
