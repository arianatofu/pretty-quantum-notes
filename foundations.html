Foundations of Quantum Mechanics

Why Quantum Mechanics Exists

Classical physics works extremely well for describing everyday objects like planets, cars, and baseballs. However, when scientists began studying very small things such as atoms and electrons, classical physics started to fail. Certain experiments produced results that could not be explained by traditional mechanics or electromagnetism.

Blackbody Radiation

One of the first problems appeared when studying how objects emit light. Classical physics predicted that hot objects should emit an infinite amount of energy at short wavelengths. This was called the ultraviolet catastrophe. Max Planck solved this problem by proposing that energy is emitted in small discrete packets called quanta.

Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect occurs when light shines on a metal surface and causes electrons to be emitted. Experiments showed that increasing the brightness of light did not increase the energy of the electrons. Instead, the energy depended on the frequency of the light.

This observation led to the idea that light behaves like particles called photons, each carrying energy proportional to its frequency.

Wave–Particle Duality

One of the most surprising discoveries in quantum mechanics is that particles can behave like waves, and waves can behave like particles. Electrons, for example, can produce interference patterns just like light.

Louis de Broglie proposed that any particle with momentum has a wavelength given by

λ = h / p

This idea helped establish the foundation for modern quantum mechanics.

Birth of the Quantum World

These discoveries forced physicists to develop a completely new framework for understanding nature at small scales. Quantum mechanics describes systems using wavefunctions, probabilities, and operators rather than deterministic trajectories.

While quantum mechanics can feel strange and unintuitive, it is one of the most successful theories in physics and forms the foundation of modern technologies such as lasers, semiconductors, and quantum computing.
