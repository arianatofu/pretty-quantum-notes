Quantum Measurement Theory

Introduction

Measurement in quantum mechanics is very different from classical physics. Observables like position, momentum, or spin are represented by operators. The act of measurement fundamentally affects the state of the system.

Observables and Operators

Each physical quantity corresponds to a Hermitian operator Ô. If |ψ⟩ is the state of the system, measuring Ô can only give one of its eigenvalues λ.

Eigenvalues and Eigenstates

The eigenvalue equation is:

Ô |φ⟩ = λ |φ⟩

Here, |φ⟩ is an eigenstate of the operator with eigenvalue λ. After measurement, the system collapses into the eigenstate corresponding to the measured value.

Probability of Measurement Outcomes

If the system is in a state |ψ⟩, the probability of measuring eigenvalue λ (with eigenstate |φ⟩) is:

P(λ) = |⟨φ|ψ⟩|²

This is why quantum measurements are inherently probabilistic.

Expectation Values

The average value expected from many measurements of an observable Ô is called the expectation value:

⟨Ô⟩ = ⟨ψ| Ô |ψ⟩

Key Concepts

  • Measurement collapses the wavefunction into an eigenstate.
  • Only eigenvalues of the operator can be observed.
  • Quantum mechanics predicts probabilities, not deterministic outcomes.
  • Repeated measurements of the same observable can give the same or different results depending on the system’s state.

Summary

Quantum measurement theory shows that observation is an active part of quantum systems, not just a passive reading of pre-existing properties. Understanding this is crucial for concepts like superposition, entanglement, and quantum information.
