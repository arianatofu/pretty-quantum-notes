Particle in a Box

Introduction

The “particle in a box” (also called the infinite potential well) is one of the simplest models in quantum mechanics. It describes a particle confined to a one-dimensional region with infinitely high walls, so it cannot escape.

Potential

The potential energy is defined as:

V(x) = 0, 0 < x < L
V(x) = ∞, x ≤ 0 or x ≥ L

Here, L is the width of the box. The particle is free to move inside, but cannot exist outside.

Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation

Inside the box, the time-independent Schrödinger equation is:

- (ħ² / 2m) d²ψ/dx² = E ψ

With boundary conditions ψ(0) = ψ(L) = 0.

Solutions

The normalized wavefunctions are:

ψ_n(x) = √(2/L) sin(n π x / L), n = 1,2,3,...

The corresponding energy levels are:

E_n = (n² π² ħ²) / (2 m L²), n = 1,2,3,...

Key Points

  • Energy is quantized – only discrete energy levels are allowed.
  • Wavefunctions are sinusoidal inside the box.
  • The probability of finding the particle is highest at the antinodes of the wavefunction.
  • The ground state (n=1) has nonzero energy – called zero-point energy.
  • This model helps understand more complex quantum systems.

Summary

The particle in a box is a foundational example in quantum mechanics. It illustrates quantization, wavefunction behavior, and the importance of boundary conditions. Many other quantum problems build on this simple model.
