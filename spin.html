Quantum Spin

Introduction

Spin is an intrinsic form of angular momentum carried by particles. Unlike orbital angular momentum, spin does not depend on spatial motion. It is fundamental for understanding electrons, protons, neutrons, and other quantum particles.

Spin Operators

Spin is represented by operators Ŝ_x, Ŝ_y, Ŝ_z that satisfy the same angular momentum commutation relations:

[Ŝ_x, Ŝ_y] = i ħ Ŝ_z
[Ŝ_y, Ŝ_z] = i ħ Ŝ_x
[Ŝ_z, Ŝ_x] = i ħ Ŝ_y

Spin-1/2 Particles

For spin-1/2 particles, the eigenvalues of Ŝ_z are:

m_s = ± 1/2

The corresponding eigenstates are often written as:

|↑⟩ and |↓⟩

Pauli Matrices

Spin-1/2 operators can be expressed using the Pauli matrices:

Ŝ_x = (ħ/2) σ_x, Ŝ_y = (ħ/2) σ_y, Ŝ_z = (ħ/2) σ_z

Where:

σ_x = [[0,1],[1,0]], σ_y = [[0,-i],[i,0]], σ_z = [[1,0],[0,-1]]

Key Concepts

  • Spin is quantized and intrinsic to particles.
  • Spin operators do not commute; you cannot know all components simultaneously.
  • Spin-1/2 particles have two possible states along any axis.
  • Spin is essential for magnetic properties, quantum statistics, and quantum information.

Summary

Spin is a fundamental property of quantum particles that influences magnetism, entanglement, and particle behavior. Understanding spin is essential for atomic structure, spectroscopy, and quantum computing.
