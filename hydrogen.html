Hydrogen Atom

Introduction

The hydrogen atom is the simplest atom, consisting of a single proton and electron. Its quantum description is a cornerstone of quantum mechanics, revealing energy quantization, orbitals, and wavefunctions.

Schrödinger Equation

In spherical coordinates, the time-independent Schrödinger equation for the hydrogen atom is:

- (ħ² / 2m) ∇² ψ(r,θ,φ) - (e² / 4πε₀ r) ψ(r,θ,φ) = E ψ(r,θ,φ)

Separation of Variables

The wavefunction can be separated into radial and angular parts:

ψ_nlm(r,θ,φ) = R_nl(r) Y_lm(θ,φ)

Where R_nl(r) is the radial function and Y_lm(θ,φ) are the spherical harmonics.

Energy Levels

The quantized energy levels of the hydrogen atom are:

E_n = - (13.6 eV) / n², n = 1,2,3,...

These levels explain the discrete spectral lines of hydrogen.

Quantum Numbers

  • Principal quantum number n = 1,2,... determines energy and size.
  • Orbital angular momentum l = 0,...,n-1 determines shape.
  • Magnetic quantum number m = -l,...,l determines orientation.
  • Spin quantum number s = ±1/2 for the electron’s intrinsic spin.

Key Concepts

  • Wavefunctions describe probability densities of the electron.
  • Energy is quantized, and transitions produce spectral lines.
  • Angular momentum and spin combine to give total angular momentum.
  • The hydrogen atom serves as a model for multi-electron atoms and quantum chemistry.

Summary

The hydrogen atom illustrates the principles of quantization, orbital shapes, and electron behavior. It is a fundamental example for understanding atomic structure, spectroscopy, and the foundations of quantum mechanics.
