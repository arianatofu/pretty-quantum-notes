Time-Dependent Perturbation

Introduction

Time-dependent perturbation theory helps us understand quantum systems under changing conditions, such as external fields or time-varying potentials.

Basic Idea

If the Hamiltonian is written as:

Ĥ(t) = Ĥ₀ + V̂(t)

Where Ĥ₀ is solvable exactly and V̂(t) is a small, time-dependent perturbation, we can compute how states evolve over time using perturbative expansions.

Transition Amplitudes

For a system initially in state |i⟩, the probability amplitude to find it in state |f⟩ at time t is:

c_f(t) ≈ -(i/ħ) ∫₀^t dt' ⟨f|V̂(t')|i⟩ e^(i ω_fi t')

Where ω_fi = (E_f - E_i)/ħ. The **transition probability** is |c_f(t)|².

Applications

  • Absorption and emission of light by atoms.
  • Rabi oscillations in two-level systems.
  • Quantum control and dynamics of qubits.
  • Fermi’s Golden Rule for constant perturbations:

Γ_i→f = (2π/ħ) |⟨f|V̂|i⟩|² ρ(E_f)

Where ρ(E_f) is the density of final states.

Key Concepts

  • Time-dependent perturbations change the state probabilities over time.
  • First-order theory gives good estimates for weak, short-time perturbations.
  • Important in spectroscopy, quantum optics, and atomic transitions.

Summary

Time-dependent perturbation theory lets us predict transitions between quantum states when systems experience changing environments. It is crucial for understanding light-matter interactions, quantum control, and dynamic phenomena.
