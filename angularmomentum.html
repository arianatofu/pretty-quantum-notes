Quantum Angular Momentum

Introduction

Angular momentum is a fundamental quantity in quantum mechanics, describing rotational motion. It comes in two forms: orbital and intrinsic (spin). Operators represent angular momentum components, and their eigenvalues are quantized.

Orbital Angular Momentum

For a particle moving in space, the orbital angular momentum operator is:

𝐿̂ = r × p̂

The components satisfy the commutation relations:

[L̂_x, L̂_y] = i ħ L̂_z
[L̂_y, L̂_z] = i ħ L̂_x
[L̂_z, L̂_x] = i ħ L̂_y

Eigenvalues and Quantization

The square of the angular momentum and its z-component have discrete eigenvalues:

𝐿̂² |l,m⟩ = ħ² l(l+1) |l,m⟩
L̂_z |l,m⟩ = ħ m |l,m⟩

Where l = 0,1,2,... and m = -l,...,l.

Spin Angular Momentum

Spin is an intrinsic form of angular momentum independent of motion in space. For spin-1/2 particles (like electrons):

Ŝ² |s,m_s⟩ = ħ² s(s+1) |s,m_s⟩, Ŝ_z |s,m_s⟩ = ħ m_s |s,m_s⟩

Here, s = 1/2 and m_s = ±1/2.

Key Concepts

  • Angular momentum is quantized: only certain values are allowed.
  • Operators for different components do not commute – you cannot know all components simultaneously.
  • Orbital and spin angular momentum combine to give total angular momentum.
  • Angular momentum rules are essential for atomic structure, spectroscopy, and quantum chemistry.

Summary

Quantum angular momentum explains rotational properties of particles, atoms, and molecules. Understanding it is crucial for spin, magnetic moments, and selection rules in quantum systems.
